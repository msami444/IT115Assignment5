Around the Water's Edge

Exploring Earth's Lakes

Lakes are some of the most captivating natural features on our planet, offering diverse landscapes and ecosystems across the globe. Lake Baikal in Russia stands out as the world's deepest and oldest freshwater lake, containing an astounding 20% of the world's unfrozen freshwater reserve. In North America, Lake Superior reigns as the largest freshwater lake by surface area, comparable in size to Austria and holding about 10% of the world's surface freshwater. Africa boasts Lake Victoria, the continent's largest lake by area, shared by Tanzania, Uganda, and Kenya.

Europe is home to picturesque bodies of water like Lake Geneva, straddling Switzerland and France, known for its deep blue waters and surrounding mountains. In South America, Lake Titicaca holds the title of the highest navigable lake in the world, situated between Peru and Bolivia. Asia features the enormous Caspian Sea, which, despite its name, is actually the world's largest lake.

These lakes not only shape the geography of their regions but also play crucial roles in local ecosystems, supporting unique flora and fauna. Many, like Lake Baikal and Lake Tanganyika, are biodiversity hotspots, harboring species found nowhere else on Earth. From the waters of Crater Lake in Oregon to the saline expanse of the Dead Sea between Israel and Jordan, lakes around the world continue to captivate and inspire with their beauty and ecological significance.
